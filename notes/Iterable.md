An [[Object]] capable of returning its members one at a time. Examples of iterables include [[Sequence]] [[Type]]s (such as [[List]], [[String]], and [[Tuple]] and some other [[Collection]] [[Type]]s like [[Dictionary]], [file objects](https://docs.python.org/3/glossary.html#term-file-object), and [[Object]]s of any [[Class]]es you define with an [`__iter__()`](https://docs.python.org/3/reference/datamodel.html#object.__iter__ "object.__iter__") [[Magic method]] or with a [`__getitem__()`](https://docs.python.org/3/reference/datamodel.html#object.__getitem__ "object.__getitem__") [[Magic method]] that implements [sequence](https://docs.python.org/3/glossary.html#term-sequence) semantics.

Iterables can be used in a [[For loop]] and in many other places where a sequence is needed ([[Zip]], [`map()`](https://docs.python.org/3/library/functions.html#map "map"), …). When an iterable object is passed as an argument to the [[Built-in function]] [`iter()`](https://docs.python.org/3/library/functions.html#iter "iter"), it returns an iterator for the object. This iterator is good for one pass over the set of values. When using iterables, it is usually not necessary to call [`iter()`](https://docs.python.org/3/library/functions.html#iter "iter") or deal with iterator [[Object]]s yourself. The [[For loop]] [[Statement]] does that automatically for you, creating a temporary unnamed [[Variable]] to hold the iterator for the duration of the loop.
